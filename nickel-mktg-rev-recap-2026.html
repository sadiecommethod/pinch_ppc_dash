March 2026 delivered The Nickel's strongest RevPAR performance since opening, with room revenue reaching $614,530 across 1,203 room nights at an ADR of $511. RevPAR grew 73% versus the prior month to $395, driven by a combination of sharply higher rates — the property priced above the competitive set's $499 ADR at $513 — and improving occupancy. Weekend demand, particularly Friday and Saturday, remains the property's commercial engine: those two nights together account for 42.3% of all room revenue at ADRs of $697 and $677 respectively. Direct-channel rate plans are also performing well, with the Pay Now & Save non-refundable rate representing 34.8% of room revenue and the Best Flexible Rate adding another 19.4%, signaling a healthy and growing direct booking mix.

Despite these gains, three issues demand immediate leadership attention. First, The Nickel's occupancy ran 12.3 points below the competitive set (77.0% vs. 89.3%), and the RevPAR Index of 88.6 is well below its 115 target — meaning the property is losing fair share on volume even while it wins on rate. Second, the paid search program carries structural risk: brand campaigns absorb 70% of PPC budget, new-guest revenue represents only 10% of PPC revenue against a 70% target, and the vendor has received a platform-generated grade of F with a score of 45/100. Third, the vendor's reported "total revenue" of $320,158 conflates booking-engine attribution with total hotel revenue — the two figures measure fundamentally different things, and this framing obscures how the property is actually performing. Each of these issues has a specific corrective action outlined in the Strategic Priorities section.

Month-over-month comparisons are the primary lens for March 2026, as year-over-year data is not yet available on the Hotel Metrics platform.

F  —  Vendor Assessment
Independent Read — March Marketing Report

The following is a claim-by-claim assessment of the vendor's March performance email against the available data. This analysis is not about finding fault — vendors structure reports to highlight what they control and are incentivized to retain accounts. The goal is to give the ownership team an independent read so their vendor conversations are more productive and data-specific.

~ Partial
"March closed at $320,158, essentially flat with February (+0.2%)"

What they said: Total revenue of $320,158, essentially unchanged from February, with bookings down 16.9% offset by ADR growth of 28.8% to $581.

What the data shows: The $320,158 figure is booking-engine-attributed web revenue — a fundamentally different number from the PMS room revenue of $614,530. The vendor is reporting only the slice of revenue their platform tracks, not the hotel's actual operational performance. Both numbers are real, but conflating them — or presenting one without explicitly distinguishing it from the other — creates confusion about how the hotel is actually performing.

What's missing: The vendor should clearly label this figure as "booking-engine attributed revenue" and note that it does not include all reservations flowing through channels outside their tracking scope. The ADR figure of $581 in the vendor report vs. $513 in the STR data ($511 in the PMS) also reflects a measurement difference — the vendor is measuring ADR of bookings made in March (by purchase date), while the STR measures ADR of stays in March.

✓ Accurate
"The Nickel Insider email delivered 23 bookings and $30,141 in revenue at a 9.7% CVR"

What they said: The Last Chance promotion sent March 22 was the highest-converting channel in March at 9.7% CVR, generating $30,141 in revenue from 23 bookings.

What the data shows: This checks out and is genuinely strong. At approximately $1,311 per booking, the email audience is booking high-value stays. A 9.7% CVR from a promotional email to a loyalty list is excellent — 3–5% is considered strong in hospitality email marketing. This result validates the CRM investment and argues for expanding the program.

What to watch: The vendor should be asked to report on list size, list growth rate, unsubscribe rate, and the percentage of email bookers who are new-to-file versus existing guests. If the list is small and stagnating, the high CVR will eventually produce diminishing returns.

~ Partial
"Brand IS loss is driven by rank (27%), not budget (12%)"

What they said: Brand campaign impression share declined 12.5 points month-over-month to 61%. IS loss is split 27% rank-driven, 12% budget-driven. The core term "The Nickel" captures 56% impression share.

What the data shows: The IS Lost to Budget figure of 12% is technically accurate and the vendor correctly identifies rank as the primary drag. However, the framing is incomplete. IS Lost to Budget at 12% means the brand campaign is only mildly budget-constrained — and therefore reducing brand spend by 15–20% would not meaningfully reduce impression share. The vendor has chosen to report this as "not a budget problem" without drawing the logical conclusion: there is surplus brand budget that could be profitably reallocated.

What's missing: The fact that IS is lost primarily to rank means the quality score improvement on brand terms (not more budget) is the right solution. The vendor should be asked specifically what actions they are taking to improve the Quality Score on brand terms from the current 7 to 9+.

~ Partial
"Performance Max and Transient combined delivered $34,941 in revenue on $5,587 in spend"

What they said: PMax + Transient campaigns generated $34,941 on $5,587 spend — a blended ROAS of approximately 6.3x.

What the data shows: The PMS campaign data shows Performance Max at $22,669 revenue on $3,158 spend (7.18x) and Non-Brand Transient at $12,273 on $2,429 (5.05x). The blended figure holds up. However, the vendor is presenting the blended number in a way that obscures the significant variance between campaigns. Multiple non-brand keyword groups (Boutique, Downtown, King Street) generated $0 in revenue on $853 in combined spend — a $853 write-off hidden within the blended "Transient" figure.

✓ Accurate
"Google Hotel Finder generated 288 calls and $13,008 in revenue, up 164.3% MoM"

What they said: Google Hotel Finder / metasearch generated $13,008 in revenue on minimal spend, with 288 calls, up dramatically month-over-month. Google Free Booking Links contributed $11,724 at zero cost.

What the data shows: This claim is accurate and represents genuinely strong performance. Metasearch ROAS of 23.5x ($16,316 / $695) is the highest-efficiency paid channel in the portfolio. The vendor correctly highlights this as a win. The key follow-up is whether they are actively optimizing the Promoted Hotels campaign — $166 was spent with no attributed revenue, suggesting the bidding configuration needs adjustment.

◎ Watch
"January and February were the two strongest brand revenue months since launch at $128,593 and $134,563"

What they said: Brand campaign revenue peaked in Jan–Feb; March declined significantly.

What the data shows: Brand PPC revenue fell from ~$134K (Feb) to $90,566 (March) — a $44K decline month-over-month. The vendor presents Jan–Feb as peaks without explaining what drove the March decline. If brand demand is seasonal, declining brand PPC revenue is expected and not a failure. If it reflects reduced impression share or conversion rate issues, that requires a different response. The vendor should be asked to attribute the March revenue decline specifically between (a) fewer brand searches, (b) lower impression share, and (c) lower conversion rate.

The current reporting structure conflates booking-engine-attributed revenue ($320,158) with total PMS room revenue ($614,530).
