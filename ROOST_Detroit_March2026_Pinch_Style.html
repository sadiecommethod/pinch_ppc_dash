ROOST Detroit
March 2026  ·  Detroit, Michigan

A month of genuine occupancy strength and market leadership — defined by a structural booking mix transformation that is compressing ADR, a digital program that needs realignment, and a dominant new group segment that tells a story worth understanding before entering the spring corporate season.

Executive Summary

The View from 30,000 Feet

March 2026 delivered $629,144 in room revenue — up +$113,275 (+22%) year-over-year — driven by an extraordinary 567 incremental room nights and the emergence of a dominant new group segment. ROOST Detroit claimed the #1 RevPAR rank in its competitive set of 7 properties, with occupancy surging to 71.4% from 56.5% a year ago and RevPAR growing 20.9% to $170.

The headline numbers, however, carry an important structural caveat. The GR SMRF segment — essentially non-existent in March 2025 at just 5 room nights — exploded to 511 room nights in March 2026, becoming the second-largest volume segment overnight. At $159 ADR, this segment is pricing 33% below portfolio average and is the single largest driver of the $11 ADR compression. Understanding who this segment is and whether the pricing is appropriate is the most important revenue management question of the quarter.

On the digital side, the paid search program faces structural challenges parallel to those in the revenue mix. Brand spend consumes an estimated 65% of PPC budget while non-brand campaigns — the property's primary tool for new-guest acquisition — are underfunded and underperforming. Website revenue fell 14.7% despite adequate traffic, pointing to a conversion funnel that needs attention entering the high-demand spring corporate season.

Revenue Manager's Read

The occupancy index of 109.8 — well above the 105.0 target — is a genuine strength and reflects exceptional demand capture. However, an ADR index of 91.1 against a 110.0 target, and a RevPAR index of 100.1 against a 115.0 target, shows the property is currently trading volume for rate. The GR SMRF segment at $159 ADR is the primary cause. Before pursuing any tactical rate adjustments, the first question must be: Is the GR SMRF segment a strategic choice or a structural pricing gap? If strategic (e.g., a specific corporate or relocation contract), the ADR underperformance is explainable. If it's accumulated OTA or walk-in demand at deeply discounted rates, it requires intervention.

GR SMRF: The Dominant New Force — at What Price?

GR SMRF exploded from 5 room nights to 511 — a 10,200% year-over-year increase — and is now the second-largest segment by volume. The $79,914 in new revenue is real and meaningful. But at $159.39 ADR — $79.61 below portfolio average — this segment is compressing blended rate substantially. Without it, March ADR would have been approximately $256, well above the comp set. The critical questions for leadership: What is GR SMRF? Is this a structured rate plan (SMERF group, monthly furnished rental, corporate relocation block)? Is the pricing intentional and contracted, or has it accumulated through opportunistic discounting? Root cause analysis is required before Q2 strategy is set.

OTA Segment: Volume Down, Rate Up — A Mixed Signal

Both OTA channels lost significant room nights: TR OTA NREF fell 133 nights (−31%) and TR OTA FLEX fell 108 nights (−71%). However, ADRs on both improved substantially — OTA NREF ADR rose $52.38 and OTA FLEX rose $79.95. This rate improvement suggests either better rate positioning on OTA channels, or that the channel mix within OTA shifted toward higher-intent travelers. Combined OTA revenue fell from $134,152 to $96,647 (−$37,505), but at better rates. The question is whether the volume loss is structural (OTA channels receiving less inventory) or demand-driven.

Corporate Segment: Genuine Growth

TR NEG CORP grew 106 room nights (+17.6%) at an improved ADR of $286.55, generating $40,861 in additional revenue. CT CORP added 156 room nights (+60%) though at slightly lower ADR. TR NEG MED added 78 room nights. Combined, corporate segments added approximately $83K in incremental revenue — the property's most valuable organic growth story. Entering April corporate RFP season, this momentum should be supported aggressively. TR LOYALTY grew 15 nights to 26 at a strong $421.56 ADR — a positive signal worth monitoring.

CT RELO: A Segment Gone Dark

CT RELO (Corporate Relocation) contributed 31 room nights at $150 ADR in March 2025 — a modest but meaningful segment — and generated zero bookings in March 2026. Relocation business can be highly cyclical, tied to specific employer programs or seasonal peaks. This disappearance should be investigated: is this a distribution issue (rate plans not visible to relocation companies), a loss of a specific account, or a seasonal pattern? Relocation is typically a high-LOS segment that would pair well with the property's extended-stay orientation.

Direct Traffic Declining: −23.3% YoY

Direct traffic (typed/bookmarked visits) fell by more than a quarter. Of all channels, direct has the highest revenue-per-session ($22.69) — visitors who arrive directly are pre-qualified brand believers with high booking intent. Losing 750+ of these sessions represents roughly $17,000 in implied revenue impact. This decline typically reflects three causes: reduced repeat guest engagement, waning brand awareness from prior marketing, or guests who would have booked direct being captured through OTA or metasearch instead. A CRM email re-engagement campaign targeting prior guests from the last 18 months should be prioritized.

The Extended Stay Page: Traffic Without Conversion

The ROOST Detroit Extended Stay landing page received 6,812 sessions — 72% of total site traffic — and recorded zero attributed bookings in the analytics. This disconnect between traffic and conversion is either a tracking/attribution issue (extended stay inquiries converting via phone or email) or a genuine conversion funnel failure. Before allocating more paid media budget to extended stay keywords, this must be resolved. If 6,812 visitors are arriving on that page and none are booking online, either the tracking pixel is misconfigured or the booking flow from that page is broken. This deserves an immediate audit.

Paid Search Performance

PPC: Misfiring Where It Counts Most

ROOST Detroit is spending approximately $9,447/month on paid search — roughly 1.5% of room revenue — a reasonable investment for a 120-apartment extended-stay hotel. The budget level is appropriate. The allocation of that budget is not. The structural challenge is straightforward: an estimated 65% of spend is defending the brand against competitors already in the auction, while the campaigns designed to reach new guests — the primary job of PPC — are operating on minimal resources and generating minimal results.

The Brand Campaign: Necessary, But Over-Fed

Brand defense is important — OTAs routinely bid on independent property names, and ceding that auction space means paying commission on guests who intended to book direct. The brand campaign at 4.30× ROAS is doing its job. The problem is scale. At $6,141 and 65% of total spend, the brand campaign has grown far beyond its defensive purpose. A well-calibrated brand campaign for a 120-apartment property in a mid-sized market like Detroit should require roughly $1,500–$3,000/month to maintain strong impression share. Anything beyond that is likely capturing organic intent that would convert without paid assistance. Recommendation: Cap brand spend at approximately 35–40% of total budget (~$3,300–$3,800/month).

Extended Stay Campaign: 14 Days of Lost Visibility

The Extended Stay campaign's IS% trend shows a near-complete blackout from approximately March 10–23 — consistent with daily budget exhaustion. At $7.42 average CPC (4.8× higher than the Hotel campaign), this campaign burned through its monthly budget in the first third of the month and went dark during what is likely peak extended-stay search volume. At a 0.00× ROAS, this campaign appears to have a conversion attribution problem — it's unlikely that 124 clicks generated zero revenue. The first step is diagnosing the conversion tracking before increasing budget. But the budget cap must be resolved immediately.

Critical Finding: IS% Lost to Rank — 81.35%

Over 81% of eligible non-brand impressions are being lost because ad rank is too low. This means ROOST Detroit's non-brand ads are losing the majority of auctions they could be competing in. Despite a reported Quality Score of 10/10 (a brand campaign artifact — actual non-brand QS is lower), bid strategy is insufficient for competitive market auction prices. The IS% lost to rank problem explains why 24,000 weekly impressions are available but only ~1,500 are converting to clicks. Increasing bids on proven non-brand keywords and auditing landing page relevance for each ad group are the two highest-impact immediate actions.

Looking Ahead

Strategic Priorities — April & Q2 2026

Entering the spring corporate travel season with #1 RevPAR market rank, strong occupancy momentum, and meaningful corporate segment growth — the fundamentals are genuinely strong. The following priorities should be actioned before end of April to protect and build on this position.

1
Identify and Price the GR SMRF Segment

511 room nights at $159 ADR from a segment that was essentially non-existent a year ago is the most important unanswered question in March's data. Before any other pricing strategy is developed, leadership needs to know: What is GR SMRF? Is this a specific contracted group, a social/SMERF group block, a monthly furnished-apartment rate plan, or a catch-all for discounted miscellaneous business? At $159 ADR the property is leaving approximately $40,000/month on the table compared to its portfolio average — if this business can bear a higher rate. Conduct a rate plan audit, pull the specific reservations under GR SMRF, and determine whether this pricing is strategic or structural. This one decision has more revenue impact than any other initiative on this list.

2
Implement Extended-Stay Rate Tiering

The 8+ night segment grew by 934 room nights to 1,424 — now 42.4% of revenue at $187 ADR. This is genuine demand, but the pricing structure is binary (transient vs. deep discount). Introduce rate bands: 1–3 nights at standard rate, 4–7 nights at a modest discount (~10%), 8–14 nights at ~15% discount, and 15+ nights at ~20% discount. This approach rewards length of stay without gifting a $187 rate to a 9-night guest who might accept $215. Even a $20 improvement in extended-stay ADR would generate approximately $28,000 in additional monthly revenue at current volumes. Present a proposed rate tiering structure at the April revenue strategy meeting.

3
Restructure PPC Allocation — Brand Defense Yes, Brand Dominance No

Cap brand search spend at approximately 35–40% of total PPC budget (~$3,200–$3,800/month) — enough to defend against OTA bidding on the property name while maintaining strong impression share. Redirect the freed budget into non-brand Hotel and Extended Stay campaigns. Fix the Extended Stay campaign's daily budget cap so it doesn't go dark mid-month. Conduct a conversion audit on the Extended Stay landing page before scaling spend further — 124 clicks generating zero attributed revenue suggests a tracking or conversion path problem. The conservatively projected upside from reallocation is meaningful new-guest revenue that brand spend categorically cannot generate.

4
Fix the Extended Stay Booking Engine Conversion Funnel

The Extended Stay landing page is the highest-traffic page on the site at 6,812 sessions — 72% of total site traffic — with zero recorded bookings. This is either a tracking failure or a conversion funnel failure, both of which are actionable. (1) Validate that the booking engine fires a conversion event on the Extended Stay page. (2) Review the booking flow from that specific landing page — is the call-to-action visible and functional? (3) Consider that extended stay inquiries may primarily come via phone/email, in which case call tracking should be implemented on that page. Resolving this one conversion gap has more near-term revenue potential than almost any other digital initiative.

5
Defend and Grow OTA Non-Refundable Position

TR OTA NREF fell 133 room nights (−31%) to 300 nights — the second-largest volume decline in the portfolio. However, ADR on this channel rose $52.38, suggesting rate positioning improved even as volume fell. This could reflect a deliberate inventory restriction decision, a rate competitiveness issue, or the displacement of OTA demand by the large GR SMRF block. Before Q2, verify that OTA channel inventory allocations are appropriate and that the TR OTA NREF rate plan is competitive on Expedia and Booking.com for the 1–7 night window. OTA non-refundable at $273 ADR is the highest-quality OTA channel and should be protected.

6
Recover Direct Traffic — CRM Reengagement Campaign

Direct traffic fell 23.3% year-over-year — the steepest channel decline in the portfolio — from a channel that generates $22.69 per session versus the site average of $13.09. Recovering 500 direct sessions represents approximately $11,000 in implied revenue. Initiate a CRM email reengagement campaign targeting guests from the prior 18 months who have not returned. Offer a book-direct incentive (early check-in, apartment upgrade, or flexible cancellation) to reactivate lapsed guests during Q2. Simultaneously, review metasearch feed alignment — direct bookings and metasearch are often correlated and may have been displaced by OTA metasearch presence.
